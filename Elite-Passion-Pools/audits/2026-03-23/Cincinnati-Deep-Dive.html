3. Root Cause Analysis

The sitelink swap triggered a Max Conversions re-learning phase that exposed Cincinnati's pre-existing structural weakness: 100% broad match keywords with zero competitor negatives.

Chain of events:

  1. 3/18 8:51pm: 6 new sitelinks added to Cincinnati campaign (same sitelinks added to NKY and Dayton)
  2. 3/19: Max Conversions enters re-learning. Algorithm starts testing new auction combinations with the updated ad format. CPC ticks up slightly ($4.66 vs $4.29).
  3. 3/20: Algorithm goes aggressive — bids $7.14 avg CPC (58% higher than pre-change) on broad terms. Spend hits $150 (2x normal daily). Budget consumed on competitor searches and generic queries. Zero conversions.
  4. 3/21: Google pulls back impressions (105 vs ~200 norm) but CPC stays elevated ($5.93). Still zero conversions.
  5. 3/22: CPC starts normalizing ($3.22). One conversion returns. Algorithm may be stabilizing.

Why NKY wasn't affected:

NKY got identical sitelink changes but has a different keyword portfolio and market dynamics. NKY's search terms are more geo-specific ("pools northern kentucky", "pool builders NKY") with fewer competitor brand searches. Cincinnati's broader market means more competitors show up in search results, and broad match pulls in every one of them.

The real problem isn't the sitelinks — it's the foundation:

  • 100% broad match keywords (no exact or phrase match protection)
  • Zero negative keywords for competitors
  • Max Conversions with no tCPA cap — algorithm can bid whatever it wants
  • These issues were tolerable when the algorithm was in a stable state. The sitelink change destabilized it, and the underlying weaknesses were exposed.
